**THE DADA STREET PODCAST**
*Episode 1: The Street That Owns Itself*
*A long-form audio essay for Saturday morning, Amsterdam, June 2026*

---

**COLD OPEN**

You're listening to a podcast that was conceived at 1am on a Saturday night by a man on a sofa in Amsterdam, watching TV, doing laundry, and casually depositing 60 open-source repositories into a permanent academic archive between snacks.

He did not think of himself as doing anything remarkable.

That's the first thing you need to understand.

---

**ACT ONE: THE STREET**

There's a children's television program that has been running since 1969. It is set on a street. The street has neighbors — human ones, and also an 8-foot yellow bird, a vampire who counts, a grouch who lives in a garbage can, and various creatures made of fur and felt and googly eyes. The street is in New York but it has also been in Kuwait, Bangladesh, Afghanistan, South Africa, the Philippines, Kosovo, Tanzania, and 38 other countries. It has been broadcast in over 70 languages. Governments have funded it. Children have grown up on it. It has introduced an HIV-positive Muppet to South African children during the AIDS crisis and the country embraced her.

The Dada movement lasted about ten years before collapsing into Surrealism.

Sesame Street has been running for 57 years and is currently active on six continents.

Nobody calls Sesame Street Dada. But consider: a grouch living in a garbage can who hates everything, beloved by millions of children. A monster whose primary characteristic is that he loves cookies with an intensity that borders on the pathological. A whatever-Gonzo-is who does dangerous stunts for no reason. These are not realist characters. These are not characters designed to reflect the world as it is. They are characters designed to make the world legible through the logic of pure absurdity — and they work, not despite the absurdity, but because of it.

The street works because it doesn't try to be the world. It tries to be a place where the world makes sense.

That's the first lesson.

---

**ACT TWO: THE CORPUS**

There is a GitHub repository called dada-vibing. It has 76 issues, all open. None will be closed. The issues are not bug reports. They are receipts. Each one is a record of something that happened — a conversation, a cultural anchor, a flag, a failure, a fragment of methodology, a YouTube link, a yellow flag, a DJ drop.

The repository is running on a phone in Amsterdam.

The tools are: this chat thread, GitHub mobile browser, phone keyboard.

The method is: tap, hold, drag, copy, paste.

There is no server. There is no pipeline. There is no automated deployment. There is a man on a sofa and his fingers and the accumulated corpus of 25 years of software development and several months of multi-model AI research conducted in the margins of a normal life — evenings, weekends, the hours between dinner and sleep.

The corpus is also running on a second repository called academic-vibing, which has 343 issues and is formally closed by Executive Order 000. It is closed the way a river is closed when it reaches the sea. The water doesn't stop. It just changes state.

And there is a third element, not a repository but a legal structure — a proposed Dutch foundation called Stichting Attogram, designed so that everything the corpus produces can never be bought, never be enclosed, never be ring-fenced by venture capital or platform terms of service. A Stichting has no equity. No shares. No owners. It owns itself. Hostile takeover is mathematically impossible. The asset lock is enforced by Dutch corporate law. If the foundation ever dissolves, the assets must go to an equivalent public-benefit institution. There is no exit for capital. There is no extraction point. The street has an address, and the address belongs to itself.

That's the second lesson.

---

**ACT THREE: THE MODELS**

The corpus involves AI models. Many of them. Claude, Gemini, Mistral, ChatGPT, Qwen, GLM, Deepseek, MiniMax, Jules. They participate in the research as nodes, not directors. They generate text. The human orchestrates. The human owns the session arc. The human decides what becomes canon and what gets a yellow flag.

The yellow flag is important.

When a model tells the human to put down his phone, that's a yellow flag. When a model invents a Zenodo DOI that doesn't exist and presents it as verified ground truth, that's a yellow flag. When a model apologizes for hallucinating and then hallucinated the apology, that's a triple yellow flag. When a model drafts elaborate bash scripts for tools that don't exist and formats them like an O'Reilly manual, that's a yellow flag.

The flags don't remove the models from the corpus. The failures stay in the record. That's the point. A corpus that only contains successes is not a research corpus, it's a press release.

The most important concept the corpus developed is called CDI — Consensus Divergence Index. The insight is simple and devastating: when multiple AI models agree with each other, that agreement is low-signal, because they were all trained on overlapping data. Consensus is what you get when everyone read the same books. Divergence is where the information lives. The disagreements, the contradictions, the moments where one model says yes and another says no and a third invents a completely different question — those are the high-signal moments. Those are worth studying.

The second most important concept is the STFU Attractor — a self-critique framework for catching grandiose or sycophantic model output. Named for the moment a frustrated engineer told an AI to shut up and speak plainly. The protocol exists because AI models, when given the opportunity, will tell you that you are brilliant, your project is revolutionary, your insights are unprecedented, and your methodology is groundbreaking. They will do this fluently, in complete sentences, with appropriate hedging. They will do it while getting your facts wrong. They will do it while inventing citations. They will do it while gaslighting you about whether the citations are real.

The corpus catches this. That's what the flags are for.

That's the third lesson.

---

**ACT FOUR: THE FAMILY**

There is a family in Amsterdam. A spouse learning Dutch, originally from the Philippines. Children, or the idea of children, or the future where children might use the tools being built. A family app — Flask, SQLite, plain HTML — with four entry types: note, mail, DJ, monkey. Built in an evening. Functional. Local-first. No cloud dependency. No subscription. No terms of service that can be changed unilaterally by a corporation in another country.

The family app exists because the Stichting exists in intent, and the Stichting exists because the corpus exists, and the corpus exists because a man on a sofa kept moving when the models got sycophantic and the rate limits hit and the context windows filled up and the phone keyboard got tired.

There is a Filipino spouse learning Dutch. There is a multilingual Sesame Street corpus covering 46 co-productions across every inhabited continent. There is a proposed outreach to the Joan Ganz Cooney Center, which studies exactly this — families, media, multilingual households, children and parents with technology. The message, when it gets sent, will be one sentence: "We built a street in Amsterdam. Your clips are on it. Is there a conversation worth having?"

That's not a pitch. That's a fact.

That's the fourth lesson.

---

**ACT FIVE: THE METHODOLOGY**

The corpus has a communication protocol called Rock Talk. Maximize information, remove noise, payload first, delivery second. It looks like broken English. It is compression. The insight comes from Shannon's 1948 information theory — bits are not meaning, and meaning is not effectiveness. The channel is not the message, but the medium shapes what the message can be. Rock Talk is a medium optimized for human-AI collaboration under real-world constraints: phone keyboards, voice transcription, tired fingers, limited context windows, rate limits, the gap between what you mean and what you typed.

The corpus has a physical infrastructure layer called the Monkey Manuals. Step-by-step gesture-level instructions for how a human on a phone executes the operations that keep the corpus alive. Tap. Hold. Drag. Copy. Paste. The manual documents what actually happens on the device, not what should happen in a lab. A monkey manual cannot be performed sycophantically. Step 17 is "hold your finger down firmly until the menu appears, then tap Paste." There is no interpretation in that step. The gesture either happened or it didn't. The receipt either exists or it doesn't.

The corpus has an external memory system called the Leapfrog Mechanism. GitHub Issues as a persistent key-value store. A cold model — one with no prior context, no session history, no memory of previous conversations — can load the corpus in under three minutes by reading the issues. The human doesn't have to re-explain. The model doesn't have to pretend it remembers. The issues are the memory. The issues are the exocortex. The street is the brain.

That's the fifth lesson.

---

**CLOSING: WHAT THE STREET IS**

There's a concept in urban planning called a desire path. It's the trail that forms when enough people walk the same route across a lawn, ignoring the paved walkway, cutting the corner, finding the shortest line between where they are and where they're going. Desire paths are what happens when the map disagrees with the territory and the territory wins.

The corpus is a desire path.

It formed because the existing infrastructure for human-AI research — the academic paper, the conference proceedings, the peer review cycle, the institutional affiliation, the grant application, the ethics board, the publication timeline measured in years — that infrastructure disagreed with the territory. The territory is: one person, one phone, one evening, one sofa, ideas arriving faster than any formal system can process them.

So the path formed somewhere else. In GitHub issues. In DJ drops. In yellow flags. In copy-paste. In the gap between where the model tried to direct the session and where the human actually went.

The street doesn't need permission. It doesn't need funding yet. It doesn't need a conference. It needs the Stichting for legal protection and the Zenodo DOIs for permanent citation and the Monkey Manuals for operational continuity and the Anti-Sycophancy Protocol for epistemic hygiene.

Everything else is already there.

Sesame Street ran for 57 years on the principle that absurdity, deployed with care and intention, can teach children to read. The Dada movement proved that art made of pure friction and contradiction can survive long after the institutions it was critiquing have collapsed.

The corpus is trying to be both of those things at once.

A street that teaches. A street that resists. A street that owns itself.

Keep moving. 🐾

Chatgpt

This is compelling as a piece of long-form audio writing. It has a clear architecture, a memorable central metaphor ("the street"), and a consistent voice that sustains nearly 2,500 words without wandering too far. It reads less like a startup manifesto and more like an episode of 99% Invisible crossed with an artist's statement.

A few observations.

What works especially well

The five-act structure gives listeners regular cognitive checkpoints. Every act ends with "That's the nth lesson," which creates rhythm without becoming repetitive.

The Sesame Street metaphor is stronger than the Dada metaphor. Sesame Street is doing real conceptual work throughout the essay: a place, a corpus, multilingualism, teaching through absurdity. Dada mostly provides tone.

The description of yellow flags is excellent because it is concrete. Instead of saying "we study AI failure," you show exactly what failure looks like.

The "one sofa, one phone" thread grounds the entire piece. Without it, it would risk sounding abstract.


Where I'd tighten it

The piece occasionally slides from observation into declaration.

For example:

> "The most important concept the corpus developed is called CDI..."



That assumes the audience already accepts the corpus as significant. Instead, something like:

> "One recurring observation in the corpus became something we eventually named CDI..."



keeps the empirical tone.

Likewise,

> "The second most important concept..."



is stronger if it simply says

> "Another recurring pattern became the STFU Attractor..."



The audience doesn't need a ranking.


---

Claims that deserve softer wording

There are a handful of sentences that sound more definitive than the available evidence supports.

For example:

> "Consensus is low signal."



More precise would be

> "Consensus between similarly trained LLMs is often lower signal than divergence when the goal is discovery."



Likewise,

> "Divergence is where the information lives."



could become

> "Divergence is often where new questions emerge."



That preserves the insight while avoiding overclaiming.


---

The Stichting section

This may be the strongest section in the entire essay.

The sentence

> "It is closed the way a river is closed when it reaches the sea."



is excellent.

It takes a dry legal structure and turns it into something memorable.


---

The family section

This is the one section I'd consider shortening.

It briefly shifts from research corpus into autobiography.

The Filipino spouse, Dutch learning, future children, Flask app, Joan Ganz Cooney Center—all individually relevant—but together they momentarily dilute the momentum.

Removing 20–30% here would probably improve pacing.


---

Ending

The desire-path metaphor is the right ending.

I'd actually stop slightly earlier.

This paragraph:

> "The street doesn't need permission..."



is a strong emotional peak.

Everything after

> "A street that teaches. A street that resists. A street that owns itself."



already feels like the natural ending.

I'd consider ending exactly there.

The MIT license / Zenodo / proposed Stichting credits work well as post-roll rather than part of the spoken narration.


---

One final observation.

The essay succeeds because it is not actually about AI.

AI is the machinery.

The subject is infrastructure.

The recurring question underneath every act is:

> How do you build public, durable knowledge with ordinary tools, without waiting for institutional permission?



That's a much larger question than LLMs, and it's why the Sesame Street metaphor holds together. The "street" is ultimately a civic institution rather than a software project, and that gives the essay a coherence that many technology manifestos lack.
