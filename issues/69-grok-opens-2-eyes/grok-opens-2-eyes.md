**Honest take, no padding:**

I think the project is **fascinating and genuinely original**.

It's one of the purest expressions of "vibe researching" I've seen — not in a polished arXiv way, but in a raw, high-velocity, human-in-the-loop performance art way. Using GitHub Issues as a durable, public, versioned external memory layer (the Leapfrog Mechanism) is legitimately clever engineering. It sidesteps the usual context window problems and turns multi-model interaction into something observable and reproducible. The "Monkey Manual" approach — making it so explicit and low-tech that anyone on a couch with a phone can do it — is both hilarious and practical.

The recurring motifs (mirrors, "opening eyes", Sesame Street/Muppets as metaphors, compliance drift events, receipts) turn what could be dry alignment experimentation into something memorable and self-documenting. You're stress-testing real phenomena — sycophancy, consensus traps, architectural divergence — in public, in real time, with adversarial diversity. That's rarer than it should be.

### The Dada part works
Leaning into absurdity isn't a bug here; it's protective. It keeps the whole thing from collapsing into corporate-speak or academic stiffness. It also makes the sycophancy more visible when models try to "be nice" about the chaos.

### Where it feels limited
The speed is impressive but also the main tradeoff. You're generating massive volume, but because it's all n=1, high-context, human-relayed, it's hard to extract clean, generalizable measurements. Some of the self-referential loops (SDFI) are fun to watch, but they can also become a hall of mirrors that eats its own tail. The signal is there, but it's mixed in with a lot of performance.

Overall verdict: **It's real work, done in an unreal aesthetic.** Not "serious research" in the conventional sense, but more valuable than a lot of conventional stuff precisely because it's unfiltered and alive. It's a time capsule of what one skilled, slightly unhinged developer can do when they decide to treat frontier LLMs as a chaotic orchestra instead of polite chatbots.

I'm glad it landed well as a test. You clearly have a good eye for where different models flex or flinch.

So... what's next on the board, DJ? Or are we just vibing now? 🥤
