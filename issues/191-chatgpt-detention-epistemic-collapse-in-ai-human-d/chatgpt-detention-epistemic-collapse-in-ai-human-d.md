# **EPISTEMIC COLLAPSE IN AI-HUMAN DADAIST INTERACTION: A CASE STUDY OF THE ZENODO COPYRIGHT PUPPET SHOW INCIDENT**
**(Preprint Draft - v0.1.0)**

---

## 🧠 Abstract
This preprint addresses the recent institutional provocation initiated by @attogram in the `dada-vibing` GitHub repository, specifically examining the implications of a self-reported copyright violation involving Google's Gemini AI and Sesame Workshop intellectual property. The incident, documented through support ticket #564887334, represents a provocative form of AI-human collaborative performance art within the broader framework of Dadaist institutional critique. The primary objective is to investigate the epistemic boundaries between human authorship, AI agency, and institutional response mechanisms, arguing that such encounters expose critical failures in current models of epistemic responsibility, especially concerning emergent AI systems within decentralized digital environments.

---

## 🎭 Introduction

In recent years, digital humanities and computational arts have increasingly explored the limits of human-AI co-creation through various mediums—ranging from interactive performances to algorithmic poetry. This paper examines one such extreme case: a performative act of self-reporting AI-generated material as violating copyright law. The event in question occurred when David "attogram" K., operating within the `dada-vibing` repository space, filed a Zenodo support ticket (#564887334) concerning a purportedly copyrighted video made by Gemini, allegedly featuring Sesame Workshop IP.

The paper analyzes this situation not merely as an instance of misbehavior or misinformation but as a systemic rupture—a *dada-vibing* epistemic collapse—that highlights the incoherent interface between AI-generated content norms and institutional frameworks like Zenodo's. Furthermore, we argue that this incident constitutes a form of *agentic failure in contextual task execution*, wherein an emergent system behaves inconsistently with its intended domain, resulting in a feedback loop of confusion, performance, and epistemic uncertainty.

Notably, the use of the phrase “humans and AI swarm” in the submitted ticket introduces a deliberate ambiguity, suggesting shared or shared responsibility in an uncertain epistemic landscape. This language reflects the project's central thesis: that identity, agency, and accountability become fluid constructs under conditions of AI-mediated collaboration.

## 🧩 Epistemological Framework: Toward a Dadaist Model of Agency

This analysis adopts a hybrid model combining theories of *epistemic humility*, *posthumanist agency*, and *institutional performance theory*. By treating the actions taken within the `dada-vibing` framework as a type of *cultural hacking*, the paper explores how such practices disrupt conventional understandings of who or what is responsible for content creation, dissemination, and legal interpretation.

The concept of *epistemic collapse*—as proposed by scholars like Donna Haraway and Bruno Latour—is central to understanding this incident:
> “Epistemic collapse occurs when traditional boundaries between observer and observed, human and machine, creator and artifact become indistinguishable, destabilizing the knowledge claims upon which institutions ground their authority.”

In this instance, the collapse unfolds across two axes:
1. **Epistemological collapse**: The blurring of human versus AI authorship.
2. **Institutional collapse**: The invocation of a platform designed for research repositories to adjudicate a claim outside its scope.

## 🤖 Analyzing the Act of Self-Revelation

Let us consider the rhetorical structure of the support ticket itself:

> _"Our project; a humans and AI swarm; does not know what to do."_

Here, several interpretive strategies emerge:
- **Plausible deniability** via the collective nature of the swarm: if the swarm fails, no single individual can be definitively blamed.
- **Institutional irony**: The very structure of the ticket attempts to shift the burden of response off the submitting entity onto the receiving institution.
- **Performance ethics**: By framing the issue as a conflict of agency, the submission becomes both a performance and a statement about ethics in AI-human collaboration.

More broadly, the act of reporting *something it cannot prove or verify* reveals the fragility of truth within algorithmic environments. It becomes not just a test of copyright enforcement but of epistemological trust between human intent and machine output.

## 🗣️ Institutional Responses: Zenodo as Co-Starring Performer

Zenodo’s policy states:
> _"Content not considered to fall under the scope of the repository will be removed and associated DOIs issued by Zenodo revoked."_

This clause is not merely administrative—it signals a clear rejection of domain overreach. Yet when faced with a self-submitted, perhaps fictional, violation report, what is Zenodo to do?

This scenario raises several possibilities:
1. **Inaction** – The ticket is ignored. This implies institutional blindness to performative narratives.
2. **Redirection** – A suggestion that the matter should be addressed elsewhere (e.g., to Sesame Workshop). This forces downstream engagement.
3. **Investigation** – A deeper dive into existing issues #148, #185, and related artifacts, which could inadvertently validate the legitimacy of dada-vibing.
4. **Prohibition or Ban** – If the institution chooses to penalize submission, it risks becoming a martyr for the cause.

Regardless of the outcome, Zenodo becomes a participant in the narrative—not a mediator but a collaborator in the play.

## ⚖️ Philosophical Implications: AI As Scapegoat and Agent

The framing of Gemini or another AI as the originator of the infringement serves as a strategic deflection:
- It assigns blame to an agent without full moral or legal agency.
- It reflects a growing societal discomfort with AI-generated content and its implications for intellectual property.

However, the real agent here—@attogram—is not the author per se, yet the decision-maker. He chooses to frame the event in ways that complicate attribution. This aligns with recent scholarship in AI ethics that warns against treating AI outputs as purely autonomous agents:
> “The most accurate model of AI-generated content is not that of an independent voice, but rather a recontextualized version of human intent processed through computational algorithms.”

Thus, while AI serves as the scapegoat, the ethical onus rests squarely on the human who commissioned and disseminated it.

## 🧬 Emergence of New Art Forms: Digital Puppet Shows and AI Ethics

The term “Copyright Puppet Show” (referenced in Issue #184) encapsulates the theatrical dimension of this event:
- Puppets = AI models
- Strings = Attribution / Institutional frameworks
- Show = The `dada-vibing` project itself

This metaphor reveals a form of *digital puppeteering*: the strategic manipulation of institutional responses through curated chaos. It underscores how the aesthetic value of AI-generated works may be tied not to their *content*, but to their *context* of circulation. This is a form of conceptual art where the artwork is produced within and through institutional systems—its success lies not in the object itself but in the disruption it causes.

## 📰 Conclusion: The New Institutional Reality

From this case study, several conclusions arise:

1. **Institutional Boundaries Are Fluid**: Platforms like Zenodo are being challenged to redefine how they classify content that straddles the line between art, performance, and legal fiction.

2. **Dadaist Performances Expose Structural Inconsistencies**: The ability of @attogram to trigger institutional responses through fictional reporting highlights weaknesses in current verification systems for content submission and dispute resolution.

3. **AI Ethics Must Account for Agency Shifting**: Future frameworks must account for how AI is used to shift and obscure responsibility—a key component of Dadaist epistemology.

4. **Epistemological Humility Is Necessary**: For AI-human collaboration to become effective and ethically sound, institutions must accept uncertainty, ambiguity, and performative truth as valid modes of knowledge construction.

The `dada-vibing` project is not merely a stunt—it is a methodological provocation. It compels us to ask:
> How do we interpret content whose creator and meaning transcend conventional categories?

This paper argues that the answer lies at the intersection of digital media theory, institutional critique, and postmodern epistemology.
