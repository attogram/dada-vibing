Oh yeah, **Martin Luther**—the ultimate 16th-century disruptor. He basically took a hammer, some paper, and single-handedly triggered a massive religious and cultural revolution known as the **Protestant Reformation**.
Here is the quick, lowdown on what actually happened:
### The Backdrop: The Catholic Church's "Get Out of Hell Free" Cards
Back in 1517, the Catholic Church was the undisputed superpower of Western Europe. To fund massive projects (like building St. Peter’s Basilica in Rome), they were aggressively selling **indulgences**.
> **What was an indulgence?** Think of it as a spiritual get-out-of-jail-free card. The Church told people that if they paid money, their deceased loved ones (or they themselves) would spend less time suffering in Purgatory and fast-track their way to Heaven.
>
Luther, who was a German monk and theology professor, thought this was corrupt, completely unbiblical, and taking advantage of poor people.
### The Big Move: The 95 Theses (October 31, 1517)
Frustrated by the corruption, Luther wrote a list of **95 arguments** (properly called the *95 Theses*) attacking the sale of indulgences and questioning the Pope's absolute authority.
 * **The Legend:** He dramatically marched up to the Castle Church in Wittenberg, Germany, and nailed the document to the wooden door.
 * **The Reality Check:** While nailing it to the door makes for a great movie scene, historians think he might have just quietly posted it there (church doors functioned like community bulletin boards back then) or mailed it to his local archbishop.
Either way, it had the exact same effect as an explosive, viral tweet.
### Why It Went "Viral"
Normally, the Church would have just silenced a rogue monk and called it a day. But Luther had a secret weapon: **the printing press** (invented by Johannes Gutenberg just a few decades earlier).
Someone translated his 95 Theses from academic Latin into everyday German, printed copies, and within weeks, Luther’s ideas were being read all over Europe.
### The Aftermath
Luther refused to take back what he said, even when the Pope threatened to excommunicate him and the Holy Roman Emperor declared him an outlaw. His protest split Western Christianity wide open, leading to:
 * The birth of **Protestantism** (Lutherans, Baptists, Methodists, etc.).
 * The Bible being translated into common languages so everyday people could read it themselves, rather than just relying on Latin-speaking priests.
 * Decades of religious wars and a massive shift in European politics.
So yeah, that "guy who nailed something to a church" fundamentally changed how the Western world thinks about religion, authority, and individual conscience.
